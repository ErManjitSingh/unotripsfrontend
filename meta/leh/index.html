About Leh & Ladakh Tour Packages

Ladakh — India's Land of High Passes— is one of North India's most striking destinations. From the ancient caravan town of Lehat about 3,500 metres to sapphire lakes and glacier-carved valleys, Ladakh offers rare high-altitude landscapes, Tibetan Buddhist culture, and roads that rank among the world's most scenic drives. UNO Trips designs Leh–Ladakh packages for every traveller — couples seeking quiet mountain escapes, families on safe, well-paced itineraries, and riders or explorers chasing legendary passes and remote villages.

Leh is the heart of the region: a lively hub of bakeries, handicraft shops, and winding lanes beneath ruined ridges. Leh Palace and Namgyal Tsemo overlook the old town, while the Shanti Stupa delivers sweeping sunrise views across the Indus valley. Most itineraries begin here so you can acclimatise gently before crossing higher passes — the single smartest step you can take before heading to Nubra or Pangong.

Nubra Valley — reached via the dramatic Khardung La approach — contrasts apricot orchards with rolling dunes near Hunder, where double-humped Bactrian camels wander under stark cliffs. Diskit Monastery, with its tall Maitreya statue, anchors the valley spiritually; sleepy villages like Sumur and Panamik add hot springs and panoramas toward the Siachen approaches for travellers with extra days.

Pangong Tsois Ladakh's most photographed lake: a long ribbon of shifting blues along the Changthang plateau, hemmed by russet mountains and prayer-flag ridges. Seasonal camps along its shores make for unforgettable nights under bright stars — though nights are cold even in summer, and permits remain essential. Pairing Pangong with Chang La or a loop toward Tso Moriri turns a standard trip into a deeper Changthang expedition for those who want fewer crowds and more silence.

Across the region, centuries-old gompas — Hemis, Thiksey, Lamayuru, Alchi — preserve murals, masked dance festivals, and daily rhythms of monks in crimson robes. Villages such as Basgo, Likir, and remote pockets toward Hanle reward slower travel with dark-sky nights and frontier landscapes. UNO Trips coordinates Inner Line Permits, trusted drivers, oxygen-aware pacing, and hotels or camps matched to your budget — so whether you are planning a Leh honeymoon, a family Ladakh holiday, or a Pangong–Nubra motorcycle circuit, you get transparent pricing and ground support from arrival to departure.

Leh travel FAQs

Quick answers for permits, season, altitude, connectivity, and paperwork—before you book your Ladakh trip with UNO Trips.

Do I need a permit to travel around Leh and Ladakh?
Indian nationals need an Inner Line Permit (ILP) for protected areas such as Nubra Valley, Pangong Tso, Tso Moriri, Hanle, and certain routes. Leh town and main highways usually do not require an ILP. Foreign nationals require a Protected Area Permit (PAP) for restricted regions. UNO Trips helps coordinate permits as part of your itinerary.
What is the best time to visit Leh?
Mid-May to September is the main season: roads to high passes and lakes are generally open, days are pleasant, and most camps and hotels operate. October–April is quieter and colder; many passes close after snowfall. Always confirm road status before travel.
How should I prepare for altitude and AMS in Leh?
Fly or drive up gradually when possible, rest on arrival in Leh, avoid strenuous activity on day one, stay hydrated, and limit alcohol. Common medicines should only be taken on medical advice. Seek help immediately for severe headache, breathlessness, or confusion—these can signal acute mountain sickness.
Will mobile and internet work in Leh, Nubra, and Pangong?
Post-paid SIMs from major Indian carriers usually work better than prepaid in Ladakh. Leh town generally has 4G, though speeds vary. Remote areas like Pangong or parts of Nubra may have patchy or no data—plan offline maps and shared emergency contacts with your UNO Trips coordinator.
What documents should I carry for a Leh–Ladakh trip?
Carry a government photo ID (Aadhaar/passport/driving licence), multiple photocopies, trip vouchers, travel insurance details, and any permit paperwork provided by your operator. Keep copies separate from originals and share your itinerary with someone at home.
